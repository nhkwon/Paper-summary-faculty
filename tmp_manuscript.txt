Abstract 7
Reliable early - stage cost estimation is essential for building retrofit planning, yet prediction remains 8
challenging because retrofit costs are often highly skewed and high - cost projects are underrepresented in 9
available datasets. Conventional single - stage machine learning models may achieve acceptable overall 10
accuracy  but frequently underestimate extreme - cost projects, increasing the risk of budget shortfalls and 11
unreliable  investment decisions. To address this issue, this  research  proposes a segment - aware transfer learning 12
framework  for building retrofit cost estimation. The framework first classifies projects into low -  and high - cost 13
segments using a support vector machine classifier and then applies segment - specific artificial neural network 14
regressors enhanced through transfer learning from data - rich to data - scarce cost segments. The framework 15
was  evaluated using a real - world dataset of 419 retrofit projects involving nursery schools and healthcare 16
facilities, with direct construction costs ranging from approximately USD 5,000 to USD 800,000. Results 17
show  that the proposed framework achieved an average R² of 0.9566 and reduced MAPE and RMSE by 46% 18
and 25%,  respectively, compared with the best - performing single - stage baseline. The framework also reduced 19
prediction  errors for high - cost projects, mitigating the systematic underestimation observed in conventional 20
models. SHAP  analysis further revealed that insulation, window replacement, HVAC systems, and renewable 21
energy measures  were key cost - driving factors. The proposed framework provides a reliable and interpretable 22
decision - support  approach for early - stage retrofit cost planning under skewed and data - scarce conditions. 23
Keywords:  Building retrofit; Cost estimation; Transfer learning; Segment - aware modeling; Decision support 24
1.  Introduction 25
As  global  awareness  of  environmental  degradation  intensifies  and  natural  resources  become 26
increasingly scarce, the construction and operation of buildings remains one of the primary contributors to 27
global energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions  [1 - 3] . According to recent studies, the building 28

2
sector accounts for approximately 40% of worldwide energy usage, with the operational phase of buildings 29
responsible for the majority of this demand  [2, 4, 5] .  This growing energy demand highlights the importance 30
of interventions for improving energy efficiency, particularly within the context of existing building stocks, 31
which often exhibit outdated systems and poor thermal performance. As a result, building retrofitting 32
approaches, such as energy - efficient renovations, and system upgrades, have emerged as a cornerstone 33
strategy for achieving effective energy consumption and sustainability of the deteriorated buildings  [2, 6 - 8] . 34
Beyond energy inefficiency,  deteriorated  buildings commonly experience structural deterioration and 35
functional obsolescence  [9 - 11] , which further diminish occupant comfort and increase maintenance costs  [2, 36
12] .  Despite the clear environmental and economic benefits, the implementation rate of retrofitting projects 37
remains relatively low. One critical barrier lies in the uncertainty associated with project  costs. Therefore, 38
accurate, reliable, and interpretable cost forecasting is essential for stakeholders to effectively evaluate 39
retrofitting feasibility, secure investment, and align technical decisions with broader financial strategies  [12] . 40
T he current cost estimation  for  retrofitting  projects  often falls short in accounting for this necessity. 41
Traditional approaches rely heavily on historical averages, cost ratios, or expert heuristics, all of which may 42
introduce significant biases and fail to capture the complex interplay of variables that influence construction 43
costs. In response, a number of studies have explored data - driven alternatives, including Case - Based 44
Reasoning (CBR)  [13] , Genetic Algorithms (GA)  [2] , and  fuzzy - based  regression model  [14] , which attempt 45
to optimize prediction by learning and identifying key feature relationships. While these models have 46
improved upon basic estimation techniques, they are often constrained by their dependence on specific feature 47
structures, limited scalability, and difficulties in handling non - linear relationships and distributional extremes. 48
To address these limitations, recent studies have employed advanced  ML  techniques such as boosting 49
algorithms  [15]  and deep neural networks  [16 - 18] , which enhance prediction accuracy by capturing complex 50
non - linear relationships between input variables and construction costs. Nevertheless, these  ML  models face 51
two key challenges. First, they are often trained on datasets with limited sample sizes  [15]  or incomplete 52
project details (i.e., limited independent variables)  [16, 18] ; in some cases, studies rely solely on generative 53
or simulated data  [17] , lacking validation from large - scale field data. These limitations increase the risk of 54
overfitting or underfitting. Second, the use of a single  ML  model struggles to provide accurate predictions for 55
various projects with a wide cost range  [19, 20] .  While acceptable performance is often achieved for cost 56
ranges that are well represented in the training data, predictions for exceptionally high - cost projects remain 57
inaccurate  [19, 20] . This shortcoming can lead to systematic underestimation and, consequently, underfunding 58

To address these limitations, this  research  proposes a segment - aware transfer learning framework for 61
early - stage building retrofit cost estimation across a wide cost spectrum. The framework aims to improve 62

cost drivers using SHAP analysis to support transparent retrofit cost decision - making.  T he key contributions 68
and novelties of this paper can be summarized as follows: 69
•  A segment - aware transfer learning framework for high - cost retrofit estimation: This  research  proposes a 70
two - stage framework that combines cost range classification with transfer learning - enhanced regression 71
for building retrofit cost estimation. By transferring knowledge from abundant low - cost projects to data - 72
scarce high - cost segments, the framework improves predictive reliability and reduces underestimation 73
risk for high - cost retrofit projects. The effects of different cost cutoffs and transfer learning strategies are 74
also systematically evaluated to enhance robustness and practical applicability. 75
•  Explainable  ML  to  support  transparent  and  expert - informed  decision - making:  SHAP - based 76

cost segments. This improves model transparency and helps practitioners understand how retrofit, design, 78
and energy - related variables affect cost predictions, thereby supporting informed decision - making during 79
early project planning . 80
•  A  comprehensive real - world  retrofitting  cost dataset for practical decision support:  The  research  utilizes 81
a dataset of 419 real - world retrofitting projects containing detailed building characteristics and retrofit 82
work quantities. Covering a broad cost range, the dataset supports robust model development and 83
enhances the practical relevance of the proposed framework for real - world retrofitting cost estimation. 84

2.2  TL  in construction 130
Recently,  TL  has emerged as an effective approach for transferring knowledge across domains to 131
specialize models for specific prediction tasks. In the construction field,  TL  frameworks have been widely 132
adopted to improve data efficiency by leveraging information from large source datasets. Typically, a source 133
model is first trained on a well - represented source domain, and the learned knowledge is subsequently adapted 134
or fine - tuned for a target domain characterized by limited data availability  [30] . 135
In the context of energy prediction, Gao et al.  [35]  utilized high - quality building datasets to transfer 136
knowledge for predicting energy consumption in buildings with scarce data, demonstrating that  TL 137
frameworks based on LSTM and CNN outperformed traditional  ML  approaches.  In concrete engineering , 138
Ford et al.  [36]  employed a large dataset of natural aggregate concrete to predict the compressive strength of 139
recycled aggregate concrete using  TL .  Similar TL approaches have also been applied to predict the lateral 140
strength of reinforced concrete columns  [37] , steel corrosion in concrete  [38] , and the compressive strength 141
of ultra - high - performance geopolymer  [30] .  Beyond regression tasks,  TL  has also been extensively applied 142
in construction - related image processing applications, including crack detection in structures  [39] , safety 143
monitoring  [40] , and risk assessment for tunnel construction  [41] .  Collectively, these studies demonstrate that 144
TL can significantly improve predictive performance in data - scarce scenarios, highlighting its potential for 145
enhancing cost prediction in high - cost retrofitting projects. 146

3.2  Model development 210
The  processed  data was used for  develop ing  two categories of predictive models: single - stage models and 211
two - stage models. The single - stage models comprise tree - based algorithms (i.e., CatBoost, XGBoost, and 212
monotonic GBM) and neural network – based models (i.e., ANN). 213
To ensure a fair and consistent comparison across models, GridSearchCV  wa s employed to systematically 214

3.2.1  S ingle  models 216
CatBoost 217
CatBoost is a GBDT - based algorithm designed for classification and regression tasks. It distinguishes itself 218
from other GBDT methods through key innovations such as ordered boosting and efficient handling of 219
categorical features  [43] . Traditional GBDTs suffer from prediction shift due to using the same data for 220
gradient estimation and model building, leading to target leakage  [44] . CatBoost addresses this with an 221
ordered boosting scheme that reduces gradient bias and overfitting. For categorical features, it replaces 222
conventional preprocessing (e.g., one - hot encoding) with a modified target statistics approach that 223
leverages random permutations and averaging across iterations  [45] . Additionally, CatBoost employs 224
oblivious trees (symmetric, balanced trees with uniform splitting rules) which enhance inference speed and 225
reduce overfitting risk  [46] . 226
XGBoost 227
With changes to the loss function and loss  optimization procedure , XGBoost  [47]  outperforms traditional 228
gradient boosting algorithms. XGBoost is versatile, supporting classification, with regression, ranking, and 229
user - defined functions, as well as distributed training across multiple machines. It is dedicated to pushing 230
boosting trees past their computational limit, allowing engineers to achieve the engineering goals of faster 231
computation and excellent performance. 232

ANN is a nonlinear computational model inspired by the structural and functional framework of the human 248
brain.  In ANNs, information is transmitted through interconnected neurons, where each input is assigned, 249
a weight representing its contribution to the output. The weighted inputs are combined and processed 250
through activation functions before being propagated to subsequent layers. Through iterative training, the 251
network adjusts its weights to minimize prediction error, and the process continues until a satisfactory error 252
level, or the maximum number of iterations is reached. 253

5.  I mplications 511
The results demonstrate that the proposed TL - enhanced two - stage model provides reliable and accurate 512
predictions, achieving an average R² of 0.9566 on the test set and substantially reducing RMSE for high - cost 513
project segments compared with conventional models. Beyond predictive performance, the framework offers 514
several practical implications for data - driven decision - support in construction management. 515
The two - stage architecture is particularly effective for early - stage retrofitting cost estimation, where detailed cost 516
information is often unavailable  ( Figure  12 ).  By separating low -  and high - cost projects, the framework reduces 517
the risk of cost underestimation in high - value projects and supports more reliable planning decisions. Correlation 518
and SHAP analyses further show that the “year of completion” has limited influence on predictions, indicating 519
temporal robustness, while cost - sensitive retrofit variables such as window, door, and HVAC replacement better 520
capture changes in material, labor, and construction practices over time. 521
Although validated on nursery and healthcare buildings, the framework is scalable to other building types and 522
regional contexts through retraining with locally relevant datasets and recalibration of cost - related variables. 523
SHAP - based interpretability also provides a mechanism to verify whether learned cost patterns remain consistent 524
across different applications. More broadly, this  research  presents a general AI - driven modeling paradigm for 525
construction analytics problems with highly dispersed target distributions, where removing outliers may result in 526
significant information loss. By integrating SVM - based segmentation, ANN regression, and transfer learning, the 527
framework effectively captures heterogeneous cost patterns, particularly in underrepresented high - cost segments. 528
However, successful application requires careful adaptation of segmentation thresholds and TL strategies to local 529
data characteristics, along with rigorous evaluation of the classification stage and post - hoc interpretability analysis 530
to ensure transparent and reliable deployment in construction decision - support systems  ( Figure  12 ). 531

6.  Conclusions 534
This  research  developed a segment - aware transfer learning framework for reliable early - stage building retrofit 535
cost estimation under skewed cost distributions. Using a real - world dataset with a highly right - skewed cost 536
distribution, projects were first classified into low -  and high - cost groups using SVM, followed by segmentspecific 537
ANN regressors. Transfer learning was applied to transfer knowledge from the data - rich segment to the data - 538
scarce segment.  Using a real - world dataset with a highly right - skewed cost distribution, projects were first 539
classified into low -  and high - cost groups using SVM, followed by segment - specific ANN regressors. TL was 540
applied to transfer knowledge from the data - rich segment to the data - scarce segment. The proposed framework, 541
including different cost cutoffs and TL strategies, was systematically compared with conventional single - stage 542
models such as CatBoost, XGBoost, Monotonic GBM, and ANN. Based on the experimental results, the following 543
conclusions can be drawn : 544
•  A ll TL – enhanced  two - stage  models  consistently  outperform  the  single - stage  models,  achieving 545
reductions of 46% in MAPE and 25% in RMSE on the overall test set. 546
•  Although two - stage models without TL are specialized for each cost segment, their overall performance 547
is inferior to that of the single ANN model due to the limited number of training samples available for 548
each regressor. 549
•  Cost cutoff selection should prioritize class balance to ensure reliable SVM classification performance, 550
rather than maximizing data availability for the pre - trained model. Under data - scarce conditions, as in 551
this  research , the mean - based cutoff represents the safest and most robust choice. 552

===== PAGE 27 =====
27
•  The balanced TL strategy is the most effective, yielding the most pronounced improvement in RMSE for 553
high - cost projects. In contrast, the aggressive TL strategy is potentially risky, particularly when combined 554
with cutoffs that induce severe class imbalance. 555
•  SHAP analysis reveals that insulation -  and energy - related variables dominate the model’s predictive 556
behavior, whereas variables related to construction year, floor area, or the number of stories play a 557
comparatively minor role. 558
In the context of limited data availability and highly heterogeneous cost distributions in construction projects, the 559
proposed framework provides a data - driven decision - support tool for early - stage retrofitting cost analysis. By 560
retaining extremely high - cost projects during model training rather than excluding them, the framework preserves 561
critical project information and improves the reliability of automated cost estimation, thereby reducing the risk of 562
cost underestimation and subsequent budget overruns compared with conventional prediction approaches. 563
Despite these contributions, the framework has only been validated on nursery and healthcare retrofitting projects. 564
